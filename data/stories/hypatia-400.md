A Christian mob pulled Hypatia from her chariot in Alexandria and killed her. She was a teacher, about 45 years old, riding through the city where students came to hear her speak.

Hypatia taught mathematics, astronomy, and philosophy in Alexandria. She edited books by Ptolemy and Diophantus so students could still study them. Her students wrote to her about instruments like the astrolabe, a tool for tracking stars, and the hydrometer, a tool for testing liquids. We do not have her own books, but we know she taught in public and advised city leaders. A student named Synesius remembered her as the person who showed him how to think clearly.

Her work helped keep Greek mathematics alive long enough to pass into Arabic and later Latin learning. We remember her at all because students and later writers recorded what the mob tried to erase.
