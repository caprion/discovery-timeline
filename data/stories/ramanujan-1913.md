At a clerk's desk in south India, Srinivasa Ramanujan copied pages of strange formulas into a letter and sent them to England. He had almost no formal training, but the pages held results other mathematicians had never seen.

Ramanujan taught himself from a single dense book of results and then pushed far past it. He spotted patterns in partitions, the number of ways to break a number into smaller parts, and in infinite series, sums that keep going forever. He often wrote the answer first and left little proof behind. Hardy later said some of Ramanujan's results defeated him completely. Ramanujan also said some ideas came to him in dreams from the goddess Namagiri, which he treated as part of his religious life.

His notebooks still feed new research in number theory and related fields. His letters also changed who the wider math world thought could produce great mathematics.
