In Baghdad, al-Khwarizmi sat inside the House of Wisdom and wrote a book for people who needed answers, not riddles. He was also an astronomer and mapmaker, not only a math writer.

Merchants had to divide inheritances. Tax officials had to total debts and land shares. These problems turned messy fast when the unknown number was buried inside the calculation.

Al-Khwarizmi showed how to solve such questions step by step. He called one move *al-jabr*, which means putting broken parts back together, and another *muqabala*, which means balancing equal terms on both sides. In plain language, he taught readers to clean up an equation until the missing number stood clear. He wrote his examples in words, not symbols, but the method is still the heart of school algebra.

Latin translators carried his methods west, and the word algebra came with them. In other books, he explained the Hindu-Arabic numerals we still use and corrected parts of Ptolemy's map of the world.
