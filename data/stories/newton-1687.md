In the plague years of 1665 and 1666, Isaac Newton left Cambridge and worked alone at his family farm. There he built the core of calculus, the mathematics of change and motion. Then he kept most of it private for about twenty years.

The big problem was motion. How do you describe a planet speeding up, a cannonball curving down, or an object changing position every instant? Old geometry was not enough.

Newton's answer was fluxions, his version of calculus. In the *Principia*, published in London in 1687, he used those ideas to connect motion, force, and gravity. But Gottfried Leibniz had developed calculus on his own and published first in Leipzig in 1684. A bitter feud followed. The Royal Society later ruled for Newton, with Newton himself towering over the process. Modern historians treat the two men as independent discoverers, and the notation most students use now, like dy/dx, comes from Leibniz.

Calculus changed physics forever. It also left one of the biggest priority fights in math history, one where the loser's notation is the one you use in class today.
