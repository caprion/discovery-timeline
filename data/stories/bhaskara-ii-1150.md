Legend says Bhaskara II named his book *Lilavati* after his daughter. One story says a water clock went wrong, spoiled the exact time for her wedding, and stayed in his family memory. The legend may not be true, but it lasted because the book did too.

People needed arithmetic they could actually use: trade, land measurement, taxes, and astronomy. They also needed better ways to solve equations that did not fall apart when zero or negative numbers appeared.

Bhaskara turned lessons into puzzles and verses. He asked for answers about heaps of pearls, swarms of bees, and patterns of numbers. Inside those playful questions sat serious mathematics: quadratic equations, combinations, rules for zero, and rules for negative numbers. In his larger body of work, he also pushed hard on tough whole-number equations that later became famous in Europe.

*Lilavati* helped keep advanced Indian mathematics alive and teachable. It was a textbook, but it sounded like a conversation. That is one reason it traveled so far.
