George Boole was the son of a shoemaker in Lincoln. He never earned a university degree. By 1854 he was teaching in Cork, Ireland, and sending a strange new book to a publisher in London.

The problem sounds almost impossible: can reasoning itself be written as mathematics? Can "true" and "false" behave like numbers you can calculate with?

Boole said yes. In *An Investigation of the Laws of Thought*, he treated logical statements as symbols and set up rules for combining them. AND keeps only what two statements share. OR joins possibilities. NOT flips one claim into its opposite. Later people rewrote these ideas with 1s and 0s, but the core move was Boole's: thought could be handled by algebra.

That idea sat quietly for a while. Then electrical engineers and computer scientists turned it into circuits. Boole gave the language. Turing later built the machine.
