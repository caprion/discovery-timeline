Emmy Noether taught in Göttingen under David Hilbert's name because the university would not give her a proper position. In 1918, while working under those limits, she proved one of the deepest links in all of mathematical physics.

Physicists knew some great conservation laws already. Energy stays conserved. Momentum stays conserved. But why should nature obey those rules?

Noether found the pattern. A symmetry means a system stays the same after a certain change. Shift an experiment in time and the laws stay the same: that symmetry gives conservation of energy. Shift it in space and you get conservation of momentum. Rotate it and you get conservation of angular momentum. She turned a pile of separate facts into one theorem.

Einstein called her work hugely important. Later, the Nazis pushed her out of Germany, and she rebuilt her life in the United States. The theorem stayed. Modern physics still leans on it every day.
