A Roman soldier found Archimedes bent over circles scratched in the sand during the sack of Syracuse. Later writers say Archimedes told him not to disturb the circles. The soldier killed him anyway.

Archimedes cared about a hard question: how do you measure a circle exactly when its edge is curved? You can measure straight lines with a ruler. A curved edge is trickier.

He attacked the circle with polygons, shapes made of straight sides. He drew one polygon inside the circle and one outside it, then kept doubling the number of sides until both had 96. The true answer for pi had to sit between them. That gave him a tight range: a little more than 3.1408 and a little less than 3.1429.

This was bigger than one good number for pi. Archimedes showed how to trap a truth between two bounds and prove it cannot escape. Later mathematicians used the same kind of thinking for geometry, calculus, and much more.
