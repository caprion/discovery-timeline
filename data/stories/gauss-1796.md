On 30 March 1796, 18-year-old Carl Friedrich Gauss made a short note in his diary. It said he had found how to construct a regular 17-sided polygon with only a compass and straightedge. He later said that day helped decide his future: mathematics, not languages.

Greek geometers had long known how to build some regular shapes, like the triangle, square, and pentagon. But nobody had added a truly new constructible polygon in more than 2000 years.

Gauss showed that the 17-gon could be built by a chain of exact steps the classical tools allow. Behind the picture sat deep number theory. The angles of the shape could be broken into parts using repeated square roots, and repeated square roots are exactly the kind of moves compass-and-straightedge geometry can make.

This was one discovery, but it tied two old worlds together: geometry and arithmetic. More than a century later, Emmy Noether would work in this same city, Göttingen, on a different kind of algebra entirely.
