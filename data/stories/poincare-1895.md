Henri Poincaré wrote so much mathematics so fast that colleagues joked he never had a first draft, only a final one. He worked in Paris in the 1890s, moving between physics, astronomy, and pure math like they were the same subject. Later in life he wrote books explaining his ideas to ordinary readers so clearly that in 1908 France elected him to the Académie française, its literary academy, an honor almost never given to a scientist.

In 1895 he asked a question that sounds almost like a joke. If you stretch or squash a shape without tearing it, how do you know what shape it really is? That question, laid out in his paper *Analysis Situs*, created topology.

Ordinary geometry measures lengths, angles, and areas. Topology cares about connectedness instead. A coffee mug and a doughnut count as cousins because each has one hole. Poincaré built tools for this new kind of thinking, including the fundamental group, which studies loops drawn on a shape. If every loop can shrink to a point, the shape behaves one way. If some loops get stuck around a hole, it behaves another. A simple test, but it lets mathematicians tell deep differences between shapes.

Poincaré also left behind a question about the 3-dimensional sphere that took 108 years to answer. Grigori Perelman finally solved it in 2003. One paper had opened a whole continent of mathematics.
