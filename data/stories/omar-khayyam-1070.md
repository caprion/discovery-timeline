Omar Khayyam solved cubic equations by day and wrote poetry by night. He was both a serious mathematician and the poet later linked to the *Rubaiyat*. The double life sounds invented, but it is real.

Algebra had good tools for quadratics, equations where the highest power is squared. Cubic equations, where the highest power is three, were much harder. A single trick did not solve them all.

Khayyam sorted cubic equations into fourteen types. Then he solved many of them by crossing conic sections, curves such as circles, parabolas, and hyperbolas. Instead of forcing one formula onto every case, he matched each kind of equation to a geometric construction. He also understood that some cubic problems could not be solved by ruler and compass alone.

That last insight was ahead of its time by centuries. Later algebra kept asking the same question in new forms: which equations can be solved, and by what methods? Khayyam had already pointed toward it.
