At 23, Aryabhata wrote a compact book of mathematics and astronomy in the Gupta capital. He wrote it in verse. In one line, he gave a value of pi correct to four decimal places.

He was working on real problems. Astronomers needed better ways to track the sky. Mathematicians needed good numbers for circles and good tables for angles.

Aryabhata wrote pi as 62832 divided by 20000, which is 3.1416. He also built a sine table. A sine is a number that helps connect an angle to a length in a triangle. By packing these rules into short verses, he made them easier to memorize, copy, and teach.

The Aryabhatiya became one of the great handbooks of Indian mathematics. Later scholars argued with it, extended it, and used it for centuries. A 23-year-old had written a book people still study 1500 years later.
