Sofia Kovalevskaya wanted to study mathematics, but Russia blocked women from traveling freely for higher education. So she entered a marriage of convenience, left, and kept going. Years later, she sent a major paper into a prize competition without her name attached.

The problem was a spinning rigid body, like a top fixed at one point. Most such motions are messy. They wobble, twist, and resist exact formulas.

Kovalevskaya found one of the rare cases that can be solved completely. She translated the spinning motion into equations involving hyperelliptic functions, a family of advanced curves and formulas. Her solution was so strong that the judges of the Prix Bordin doubled the prize money. They had no idea the author was a woman until after the result.

She later became a professor in Stockholm, the first woman in modern Europe to hold a full mathematics professorship. In mechanics, people still speak of the Euler top, the Lagrange top, and the Kovalevskaya top.
