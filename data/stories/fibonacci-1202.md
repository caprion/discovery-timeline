Fibonacci grew up around trade. His father worked in Bugia, in North Africa, and young Leonardo learned calculation there. When he came back to Pisa, he brought home what he called the art of the nine Indian figures.

Europe still leaned on Roman numerals. They were fine for carving dates into stone. They were awful for multiplication, division, and bookkeeping.

In *Liber Abaci*, Fibonacci showed how place value works. In place value, the same digit means different things depending on where it stands. A 2 can mean two ones, two tens, or two hundreds. He walked readers through money, weights, exchange rates, and profits. He also slipped in a rabbit problem whose number pattern later took his name.

The book did not erase Roman numerals overnight. But it gave merchants and teachers a better tool, and better tools spread. The numbers on your keyboard are part of that story.
