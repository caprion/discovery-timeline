Leonhard Euler stared at a map of Königsberg, a city in Prussia, and asked a strange question. Could a person cross all seven bridges exactly once and finish the walk without repeating any bridge? Euler had never even visited the city.

At first the problem looks like a puzzle about streets and rivers. It feels local. It is really about connections.

Euler stripped the map down to its skeleton. He treated each land area as a point and each bridge as a line between points. Then he counted how many bridges touched each point. If too many points have an odd number of bridges, a one-time walk is impossible. Königsberg had that problem, so the walk could not be done.

That move changed math. Euler showed that shape and distance sometimes do not matter. Connection matters. From one city map came graph theory, and from there a road toward topology.
