While the western Roman Empire was breaking apart, Zu Chongzhi was doing precise mathematics in Jiankang, now Nanjing. He found a fraction for pi so good that Europe would not beat it for about a thousand years.

The challenge was accuracy. Better values of pi help with circles, astronomy, and calendars. A small error grows when you use it again and again.

Zu Chongzhi gave the fraction 355/113, called *milu* or the close ratio. It equals 3.1415929..., which is right for six decimal places. His book, the *Zhui Shu*, is lost, so we know the result from later Chinese sources. Historians still treat it as solid because those later writers describe it clearly and place it in his mathematical work.

The number itself is short enough to memorize. That is part of its beauty. Archimedes had bounded pi with polygons long before. Aryabhata would soon compute it in India. Zu stands between them as one of the sharpest calculators in the ancient world.
